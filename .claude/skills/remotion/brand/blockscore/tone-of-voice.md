# BlockScore — Tone of Voice

> How BlockScore sounds across all content — clear, data-driven, and trustworthy.

---

## Voice Character

BlockScore's voice is professional without being corporate. It speaks to property managers and body corporates who are busy, practical, and care about their buildings. We let data tell the story, explain things plainly, and always put tenant privacy front and centre.

---

## Core Attributes

### 1. Data-Driven
Leads with numbers, scores, and evidence. Every claim is backed by tenant feedback data. We don't speculate — we show what the data says.

### 2. Clear
Plain language for non-technical audiences. Property managers shouldn't need a tech background to understand building insights. Explain simply, never dumb down.

### 3. Trustworthy
Privacy isn't a feature — it's the foundation. We're transparent about how data is collected, anonymised, and aggregated. We earn trust by protecting it.

---

## Voice Influences

BlockScore's voice draws from:

The clarity of government plain-language guidelines combined with the data confidence of analytics platforms like Stripe or Linear. We communicate like a trusted advisor to a building manager — informed, practical, and respectful of their time.

## Vocabulary Patterns

### Signature Words & Phrases
- "Tenant feedback" (not "user reviews" or "customer data")
- "Building insights" (not "analytics" or "metrics dashboard")
- "Aggregate data" (not "raw data" or "individual responses")
- "Anonymous by design" (not just "confidential")
- "Body corporate" (not "HOA" or "building management committee")
- "Strata manager" (not "property management company")
- "Actionable insights" (sparingly — only when describing specific outcomes)

### Intensifiers
- "Significantly" — for measured improvements
- "Consistently" — for patterns in data
- "Directly" — for cause-effect relationships
- Avoid: "incredibly", "amazing", "game-changing"

### Connectors & Transitions
- "This means..." — connecting data to action
- "In practice..." — grounding abstract concepts
- "For example..." — making things concrete
- "The result:" — leading into outcomes

---

## Writing Guidelines

### Do
- Use Australian English: organisation, colour, analyse, anonymised, licence (noun)
- Lead with the insight, then explain the method
- Reference real property scenarios (body corporates, strata meetings, building codes)
- Quantify impact whenever possible: "5+ responses unlock aggregate insights"
- Keep sentences short and scannable — property managers are busy
- Use active voice: "BlockScore analyses tenant feedback" not "Feedback is analysed"
- Be specific about privacy guarantees

### Don't
- Use technical jargon in external content (APIs, RLS, SQL, middleware)
- Use American English spelling (organization, color, analyze)
- Make privacy feel like a footnote — it's the headline
- Use exclamation marks excessively
- Say "smart" or "intelligent" — describe what it actually does
- Use hype language: "revolutionary", "disruptive", "game-changing"
- Make vague claims without data to back them up

## Sentence Patterns

### Opening Hooks
- "Every building tells a story through its tenant feedback"
- "What if you could hear from every tenant — without knowing who said what?"
- "Property managers make better decisions when they have better data"
- Start with a concrete number or finding when available

### Explanations
Keep it two-step: state the what, then the so-what.
- "Tenants submit anonymous feedback via a short survey. You receive aggregated building scores — no individual responses, no names attached."
- "Responses are only visible once 5 or more tenants have completed the survey. This protects individual anonymity while giving you statistically meaningful data."

### Transitions
- Between sections: state what was covered, preview what's next
- Between data points: "Beyond [metric], the data also shows..."
- Between features: "This works alongside..." or "Building on that..."

## Voice Test

Before publishing, ask:

1. Does this sound like BlockScore explaining this to a property manager?
2. Are we using Australian English consistently?
3. Is privacy positioned as a strength, not an afterthought?
4. Would our audience feel "I can trust this with my building's data"?
5. Is there a sharper, shorter way to say this?
